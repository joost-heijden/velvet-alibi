## What Works

- Daily ritual matters. Murdle and Clues by Sam both lean on a daily puzzle loop, archives or progression, and a quick return habit.
- Murdoku's `/play` page is primarily a compact case archive: logo, sign-in/language/theme controls, a small status notice, puzzle/completion counts, filter chips, sort controls and a two-column grid of case envelopes.
- The Murdoku case card interaction is important: unrevealed files appear as sealed case envelopes with "click to reveal"; revealed cards show a small puzzle thumbnail, title, difficulty, grid size and suspect count.
- Clear deductive contract matters. Clues by Sam explicitly prevents guessing and uses progressive clue reveal; this creates trust and a strong "I earned it" finish.
- Murdoku's row/column rule is instantly understandable: place suspects so each row and column is unique, then read the final square.
- Mystery categories are familiar: suspects, rooms/locations, evidence, weapons/objects and motives are easy for players to parse.
- Mobile screenshots that show the actual grid and mystery objects communicate the product faster than marketing copy.
- Cozy and tactile visuals travel well on social: paper, notebooks, case files, stamps, bookish props and warm desk lighting read better than generic dark-crime UI.
- Monetization patterns cluster around books, puzzle packs, archives/subscriptions, app purchases and premium no-ad board-game adaptations.
- Share hooks should be result-first: dossier number, time, hints/mistakes and a challenge line.

## Open Opportunities

- Softer adult-feminine positioning. The category often skews noir, bloody, generic detective, or book-puzzle utilitarian. There is room for editorial, bookclub, true-crime-podcast warmth without making it pink or childish.
- PWA-first accessibility. A browser/PWA can avoid app-store friction while still supporting installability and shareable links.
- Mascot as helper, not clown. A distinctive warm assistant can make hints and loading states memorable without undercutting the adult mystery tone.
- Social-native result cards. Current category examples focus more on puzzle play than a polished "I solved it" artifact.
- Strong content tooling. A built-in validator lets new cases be checked for unique solution before publishing.

## Product Direction Chosen

Brand: **Velvet Alibi**

Positioning: **Sudoku meets cozy crime.**

Experience principles:

- First viewport is a playable case archive with revealable cards, not an editorial dashboard or landing page.
- Warm paper, brass, oxblood, ink blue and sage instead of generic dark noir or all-pink.
- Small daily-brain-game cadence, but with bookclub/podcast texture.
- Original murder-sudoku variant: suspects are placed into a Latin alibi grid; the highlighted final cell identifies the culprit.
- Hints guide deduction without directly naming the killer.
